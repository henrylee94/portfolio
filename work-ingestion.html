Transaction-record ingestion — over ten million records a day from 10+ sources

Every balance change a user sees, gathered from 10+ systems, always adding up.

over ten million records/day 10+ sources hot / cold split

Situation

Every balance change a user sees must be complete, ordered, and fast to query — but records originate from 10+ heterogeneous sources that disagree on shape, timing, and delivery guarantees: millions/day through the audited workflow path, the rest streaming directly from providers and internal systems.

Design

Split storage by read pattern: hot recent history on the OLTP store with covering indexes; long-window analytics offloaded to a columnar layer fed by the ingestion pipeline; source-specific shapes normalized at the boundary, with per-source caching and channel-aware routing.

Outcome

over ten million records/day ingested and queryable; recent-history reads stay fast while analytical queries stopped competing with production traffic.

War stories

Most of the work was comparing numbers

Nobody tells you this about building an ingestion pipeline: the code is the quick part. For a long stretch of development, one record subtype ran as a permanent probe — continuously reconciled against its sources while everything else was still moving.

Then the cross-checks widened: daily totals per record type compared against the analytics team's independent numbers, and per-user daily histories diffed record by record. When a figure disagreed, that was the day's work — trace which source dropped, duplicated or reshaped it, fix the boundary, backfill, compare again.

It's manual, it's unglamorous, and it's the only reason the number on a user's screen can be trusted. Correctness isn't a property you declare; it's one you keep re-earning.
