Multi-Currency & Crypto

Payment integration where a precision slip is a real loss — deposits, withdrawals and transfers threaded through every transaction boundary.

Role

Drove the payments side for two years as half of a two-engineer pair, alongside the team's assistant lead.

Scale

Every payment flow on the platform — deposits, withdrawals, transfers — with fiat↔USDT conversion at every transaction boundary.

Stack

Three wallet protocols — OMNI · ERC-20 · TRC-20 — exact-decimal balance handling, config-driven currency paths.

Outcome

Two years in production across three wallet protocols; later currencies reused the same config-driven paths instead of re-opening them.

Problem

The platform needed cryptocurrency deposits and withdrawals alongside fiat. That is not one feature — it is a conversion concern at every transaction boundary: every deposit, withdrawal and transfer now has two currencies on either side of it, and the amounts have to agree exactly.

Two things made it hard. First, provider heterogeneity: some third-party providers could not hold crypto at all, so a USDT balance had to become fiat before it could touch them — and become USDT again on the way back. Second, precision: at a wallet edge, a rounding error is not a display bug, it is a real discrepancy against a real balance.

Constraints

  • Financial correctness — a conversion at any boundary must reconcile exactly; there is no acceptable rounding drift on a balance.
  • Provider heterogeneity — some providers could not hold USDT at all, and their limitations could not be allowed to leak into every call site.
  • On-chain reality — confirmations arrive more than once; a replayed notification must never credit twice.
  • Three wallet protocols — OMNI, ERC-20 and TRC-20 each with their own transfer semantics, behind one platform-facing interface.
  • Longevity — a two-year epic, so the design had to absorb currencies added after us without re-opening the payment paths.

Role and responsibility

I drove the payments side for two years as half of a two-engineer pair, alongside the team's assistant lead. My part: the wallet protocol integrations (OMNI, ERC-20, TRC-20), USDT deposits and withdrawals, and threading fiat↔USDT conversion through every transaction boundary. The judgment call that shaped the module — absorbing provider differences in one cross-cutting compatibility layer instead of at every call site — was mine.

Architecture

USDT enters and leaves through the wallet protocols; everything in the middle is a boundary question. Each transaction boundary knows how to convert between fiat and USDT with exact-decimal math, and a single compatibility layer answers the question "what currency does this provider actually settle in?" — so a provider that can't hold USDT is handled in one place, invisibly to callers. Deposit crediting is a guarded state machine, so replayed on-chain confirmations are structurally a no-op.

Transaction paths

Deposits: on-chain detection → confirmation → credit, each a guarded state transition. Withdrawals: platform balance out to a wallet address. Transfers: balance movement across providers — where conversion fires if the destination can't hold USDT.

Walkthrough — one transfer

USDT-capable provider: the amount passes through unconverted. Fiat-only provider: the compatibility layer converts at the boundary — exact integer minor-unit math, no float anywhere near a balance — and converts back on the return leg. Replayed confirmation: the credit state machine rejects the illegal transition; nothing is written twice.

Key decisions

One compatibility layer, not per-call-site handling

Options
Let each payment flow check whether its provider can hold USDT and convert locally, or absorb the difference in one cross-cutting layer.
Decision
One layer. Every call site asks "what currency does this provider settle in?" and gets a converted-or-passthrough answer.
Reason
Provider heterogeneity was the recurring hazard; leaked into every call site, it would have been reimplemented — and mis-implemented — dozens of times.
Trade-off
The layer is on every payment path, so it carries concentrated blast radius and had to stay small and heavily reviewed.

Exact-decimal math at the wallet edges

Options
Floating-point with careful rounding, a decimal library everywhere, or exact integer minor-unit arithmetic at the boundaries where value changes representation.
Decision
Exact integer minor units at every conversion — no float ever touches a balance.
Reason
At a wallet edge a rounding error becomes a real discrepancy against a real balance. Reconciliation only works if both sides of a conversion are exact by construction.
Trade-off
Rates are carried as ratios and conversion code is stricter to write; every boundary pays a small verbosity tax for exactness.

Credit as a guarded state transition

Options
Deduplicate replayed on-chain confirmations with a processed-set check, or make crediting a state machine where a replay is an illegal transition.
Decision
State machine: detected → confirmed → credited, terminal at credited. A second "confirmed" changes nothing; an illegal jump throws.
Reason
On-chain confirmations arrive more than once by nature. A bolted-on check can be forgotten on a new path; a guarded transition cannot.
Trade-off
Every new deposit variant has to be expressed as states and transitions first, which is slower to prototype than an ad-hoc handler.

Failure modes and recovery

On a payment path, the failure modes are known in advance — the design's job is to make each one structurally harmless.

The confirmation that arrives twice

On-chain confirmations are delivered more than once — that's the transport's nature, not an edge case. Crediting is therefore a state transition guarded by the current state: detected → confirmed → credited, and credited is terminal. A replayed notification attempts an illegal transition and is rejected before it can write; the balance moves exactly once no matter how many times the network repeats itself.

The cent that goes missing at a boundary

Every fiat↔USDT conversion is a place where two ledgers must agree. Floating-point math would let them drift apart silently and surface weeks later as an unreconcilable difference. Conversion runs in exact integer minor units with rates as ratios — so when the two sides are compared, they match by construction, and a mismatch means a real problem, not arithmetic noise.

The provider that can't hold USDT

Some third-party providers couldn't hold crypto at all. Handled naively, every flow touching such a provider would need its own conversion logic — and each copy is a chance to get it wrong. Instead the difference is absorbed once, in the compatibility layer: callers move value; the layer decides whether that value crosses the boundary as USDT or as converted fiat, and converts back on the return leg.

The currency added after us

The rails were built over two years, but currencies keep arriving after the epic closes. Because the payment paths are config-driven — currency behaviour is data, not branching code — later currencies were added by configuration rather than by re-opening deposit, withdrawal and transfer logic that had already earned its production trust.

Representative code

Rewritten for publication to show the production design — not company source.

deposit-state.ts 
// On-chain confirmations arrive more than once. Credit is a state
// transition guarded by the current state — a replayed webhook is a no-op.
const NEXT: Record<State, State[]> = {
  detected:  ['confirmed'],
  confirmed: ['credited'],
  credited:  [], // terminal — a second "confirmed" changes nothing
};

function transition(from: State, to: State): State {
  if (!NEXT[from].includes(to)) throw new Error(`illegal ${from}→${to}`);
  return to; // illegal jumps throw instead of double-crediting
}
wallet-decimal.ts 
// At a wallet edge a rounding error is a real discrepancy. Never float —
// convert in integer minor units, and absorb provider quirks in one layer.
function toUsdt(fiatMinor: bigint, rate: Rate): bigint {
  return (fiatMinor * rate.num) / rate.den; // exact integer math
}

// Some providers can't hold USDT at all — the difference is
// absorbed here, not leaked into every call site.
function settleCurrency(p: Provider): Currency {
  return p.supports('USDT') ? 'USDT' : convertAtBoundary(p.fiat);
}

Testing and validation

Same discipline as the rest of the platform: Jest for the units that matter — the state machine's transition table and the exact-decimal conversion math — and integration tests against real dependencies via Testcontainers rather than mocks. Payment paths sit inside the weekly regression run, so every release re-exercises deposits, withdrawals and transfers, including the replayed-confirmation case: fire the same confirmation twice, assert the balance moved once.

Trade-offs

  • The compatibility layer sits on every payment path — one place to fix provider differences, but also one place whose defects touch everything, so it moves slower than the code around it.
  • Exact integer minor-unit arithmetic makes every conversion more verbose than a float multiply; that tax is paid at every boundary, deliberately.
  • Expressing crediting as a state machine means new deposit variants are slower to prototype — states and transitions first, behaviour second.
  • Two engineers over two years is a long, narrow investment; the payoff only shows when later currencies ride the same rails for free.

Outcome

  • Crypto rails live across every payment flow — deposits, withdrawals and transfers.
  • Two years in production across three wallet protocols: OMNI, ERC-20, TRC-20.
  • Replayed on-chain confirmations are structurally harmless — credit happens exactly once, guarded by state.
  • Conversions reconcile exactly — no float ever touches a balance at a wallet edge.
  • Later currencies reused the same config-driven paths instead of re-opening them.

What I learned

Provider heterogeneity is where payment integrations rot: the moment one call site starts special-casing a provider, every call site eventually will. Absorbing the difference in one layer was the single decision this module's longevity rests on. And precision is not a detail to handle later — at a wallet edge, exactness has to be a property of the arithmetic itself, because a rounding error there isn't a bug report, it's a discrepancy against a real balance that someone has to reconcile by hand.
