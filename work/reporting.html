Reporting Engine

Built and ran the platform's reporting for six years, before a dedicated BI team existed — multi-view analytics over million-row datasets, plus regulatory filings delivered on time.

Role

Owner of the reporting function — designed the engine, ran it in production for six years, and delivered every reporting change through it.

Scale

Millions of rows a day; long-window queries over million-row datasets; multi-million-row regulatory filing days.

Stack

MongoDB aggregation pipelines · precomputed daily summaries · batch settlement workers · streamed FTP delivery.

Outcome

A working BI function before a BI team existed; 140+ reporting changes over six years; regulatory filings on schedule at million-row scale.

Problem

Operations, partners, and management all needed data — analytics across multiple views, aggregated over long windows, on the production database — and there was no business-intelligence team to build it. Later, a regulator added a harder requirement: daily filings in exact formats, millions of rows per day, against a hard deadline.

The two workloads pull in opposite directions. Analytics wants low-latency answers over long windows; regulatory delivery wants complete, correct, multi-million-row files that fit a fixed window. Both had to run against the same primary database without degrading it.

Constraints

  • Correctness — reporting numbers feed commercial reconciliation, so a fast-but-wrong answer is not an option.
  • Late corrections — figures can change after the day closes; a frozen snapshot would lock in wrong numbers.
  • Hard deadlines — regulatory filings are due daily, in exact formats, regardless of how large the day was.
  • Shared infrastructure — long-window queries run against the primary database and must not starve production traffic.
  • No dedicated team — one engineering function had to cover operational, analytical, and compliance reporting.

Role and responsibility

I built and ran the platform's reporting for six years, before a dedicated BI team existed. That meant owning the whole surface: multi-view analytics for operations, partners, and management; operational reporting; and the regulatory filing pipeline with its daily deadline. Every one of the 140+ reporting changes over that period shipped through this engine. When a formal BI team was eventually stood up, it built on these foundations.

Architecture

A precompute + recompute dual track. An independent settlement process streams per-user transaction flows to workers in batches and lands precomputed daily summaries — the materialized layer reads are served from. A recompute window stays open behind the current day, so late corrections still land instead of freezing a wrong number. Ad-hoc queries run tuned aggregation pipelines; the scheduler was rewritten to make the 2-day recompute window reliable. Regulatory delivery is a separate pipeline off the settled data: million-row-per-file slicing with append-based FTP upload, streamed so multi-million-row days fit the filing window.

Query flow

A report request splits at the recompute cutoff: settled days are read straight from precomputed summaries; the recent window is aggregated live; the two tracks merge into one answer. Fast where the data is stable, correct where it is still moving.

Aggregation strategy

Precompute what repeats — daily summaries per view, landed by batch settlement workers off per-user transaction streams. Only ad-hoc shapes hit the primary database live, through tuned aggregation pipelines rather than raw long-window scans.

Regulatory delivery

Filing days stream in batches from the settled data, roll to a new file at a million rows, and upload by FTP append — constant memory, tuned until multi-million-row days fit the delivery window.

Key decisions

Precompute + recompute, not one or the other

Options
Query everything live on the primary database, freeze daily snapshots and never revisit them, or run both tracks — precomputed summaries plus a live recompute window.
Decision
The dual track: read precomputed daily summaries for latency, keep a recompute window open so late corrections still land.
Reason
Reporting numbers feed commercial reconciliation. Pure live queries can't hold latency over long windows at million-row scale; frozen snapshots lock in wrong numbers when figures move after the day closes.
Trade-off
Two read paths and a merge at the cutoff — more moving parts than either extreme, and a recompute schedule that has to run reliably.

An independent settlement process, not read-time computation

Options
Compute summaries on the read path when a report is requested, or run a separate settlement process that streams per-user transaction flows to workers in batches and lands the summaries ahead of time.
Decision
The independent settlement process. Reads serve materialized summaries; the heavy aggregation happens once, off the request path.
Reason
Long-window aggregation at read time would put the full cost on every request and on the primary database at the worst moment — when someone is waiting.
Trade-off
Freshness depends on the scheduler, which became load-bearing — it had to be rewritten to make the 2-day recompute window reliable.

Stream and slice filings, never buffer a day

Options
Build each filing day as one file in memory and upload it whole, or stream settled rows in batches, slice at a million rows per file, and upload by FTP append.
Decision
Stream, slice at one million rows per file, append-upload.
Reason
A multi-million-row day has to fit a fixed filing window under constant memory; buffering the whole day doesn't scale with the data and gives no room to tune.
Trade-off
Batch sizes became a tuning surface — throughput had to be adjusted until the largest days fit the window — and append-based upload needs care around partial writes.

Failure modes and recovery

The design exists to absorb three specific failure modes. Late corrections: figures that change after a day closes land through the open recompute window instead of silently diverging from a frozen summary. Unreliable recompute runs: the original scheduler couldn't be trusted with a window that reconciliation depends on, so it was rewritten until the 2-day recompute cycle ran reliably. Oversized filing days: when a day's volume grows past what a single pass can handle, the answer is in the pipeline's shape — streamed batches, per-file slicing, and append upload were tuned until multi-million-row days still fit the delivery window.

Representative code

Rewritten for publication to show the production design — not company source.

report-read.ts 
// Reporting feeds reconciliation — it has to be fast AND correct. Read
// precomputed daily summaries for speed; keep a recompute window open so
// late corrections still land instead of freezing a wrong number.
async function report(view: View, from: Date, to: Date) {
  const settled = await summaries.find({ view, day: { $lt: recomputeCutoff() } });
  const recent  = await aggregateLive(view, recomputeCutoff(), to); // 2-day window
  return merge(settled, recent); // precompute + recompute, both tracks
}
regulatory-filing.ts 
// A multi-million-row day has to fit the filing window. Stream in batches,
// slice at a million rows per file, append-upload — never buffer it all.
const ROWS_PER_FILE = 1_000_000;

async function fileDay(day: string) {
  let part = 0, rows = 0, out = openSlice(day, part);
  for await (const batch of streamSettled(day)) {
    await out.appendUpload(batch); // FTP append — constant memory
    if ((rows += batch.length) >= ROWS_PER_FILE) {
      out = openSlice(day, ++part); rows = 0; // roll to the next file
    }
  }
}

Testing and validation

Reporting follows the platform's testing practice: Jest with integration tests against real dependencies via Testcontainers, plus a weekly regression pass. The properties that matter here are the seams — that the merge at the recompute cutoff neither drops nor double-counts a day, and that the filing slicer rolls files at exactly the row limit.

filing-slicer.spec.ts 
// The slicing boundary is the contract: every row delivered, no file over the limit.
it('slices a 2.5M-row day into three files, none above the limit', async () => {
  const uploads = await fileDay(seededDay({ rows: 2_500_000 }));

  expect(uploads.files).toHaveLength(3);
  expect(uploads.files.every((f) => f.rows <= 1_000_000)).toBe(true);
  expect(uploads.totalRows).toBe(2_500_000); // nothing dropped at the seams
});

Trade-offs

  • The dual track buys speed and correctness at the cost of two read paths — settled summaries and live aggregation — that have to agree at the cutoff, forever.
  • Precomputation ties report freshness to the settlement schedule; the scheduler became critical infrastructure and had to be rewritten to earn that role.
  • Streaming with append-based upload keeps memory constant but makes delivery a tuning exercise: batch sizes were adjusted until the largest days fit the filing window, and would need retuning if volume kept growing.
  • Everything runs against the primary database rather than a dedicated analytics store — workable at this scale with tuned pipelines and precompute, but it is a ceiling, not a permanent answer.

Outcome

  • The platform had a working BI function before it had a BI team; the formal team later built on these foundations.
  • Regulatory filings shipped on schedule at million-row scale — millions of rows a day, hard deadlines met.
  • 140+ reporting changes delivered over six years across operational, analytical, and compliance reporting.
  • Reads served from precomputed summaries instead of long-window scans, with late corrections still landing through the recompute window.

What I learned

When numbers feed reconciliation, speed and correctness aren't a trade to make — the architecture has to buy both, and the dual track is what that costs: precompute for latency, a recompute window for truth. The unglamorous parts carry the system — the scheduler and the batch tuning got no design credit, but the deadlines were met or missed there. And reporting built under constraint doesn't have to be throwaway: done with clear seams, it becomes the foundation the dedicated team inherits rather than the mess they replace.
